## Garcia, "Critique! Critique! Critique! Black Labor in the Early American Book Trade"

**Full citation:** Garcia, John J. "Critique! Critique! Critique! Black Labor in the Early American Book Trade." *Criticism* 64, nos. 3–4 (Summer/Fall 2022): 317–32. Special issue: *New Approaches to Critical Bibliography and the Material Text*.

**Core argument:** Recovering Black labor in the early American/Caribbean book trade requires more than adding new subjects to bibliography's existing categories (author, editor, printer, publisher) — it requires bibliography to incorporate philosophical *critique* (Kant → Foucault → Butler) as a method, because the discipline's own presuppositions (what counts as evidence, whose labor counts as "the trade") are what occluded this labor in the first place. Garcia proposes "negative bibliography," drawing on Adorno's negative dialectics and Stephen Best's historicism of "knowing what withholds itself from the possibility of being known," as a way to do reparative scholarly work without either replaying archival violence uncritically or overclaiming knowledge the sources don't support (explicitly distances this from "critical fabulation").

**Structure:**

1. **Introduction** — states the manifesto directly: critical bibliography must critique its own field formation, not just widen its subject list. Notes the Andrew W. Mellon Society of Fellows in Critical Bibliography (Garcia is a member) avoids the word "critique" in its own mission statement — an internal jab at the field he's positioned within.
2. **"The Caribbean Reader"** — argues the British West Indies have been excluded from early American book history despite outsized print import volumes (only New England imported more books). Recovers enslaved domestic labor facilitating white plantation reading (Thistlewood's diaries), the first Jamaican printer (Robert Baldwin), and printer Alexander Aikman's own 1794 admission that production was slowed because "part of the business [was] being executed here by negroes" — direct documentary evidence of enslaved compositors. Centers a unique surviving illustrated book, *Excursion into the Interior from the Cape of Good Hope* (Jamaica, ca. 1799–1802, **held at AAS**), whose woodcuts stage colonial reading and critique together. Explicitly names AAS's own 1983 *Printing and Society in Early America* volume as having failed to synthesize prior Caribbean book-trade scholarship (Roderick Cave's work) — a critique of his own institution's founding paradigm.
3. **"Paper Trails"** — turns to US financial paperwork (ledgers, receipts, indentures) as an archive of Black labor that doesn't require literacy to recover. Key cases: two unnamed Black men paid to pull Philadelphia's 1788 Constitution-ratification "Printer's Float"; Mathew Carey's unnamed "resolute negro pressman" who struck over unpaid wages; retired 94-year-old Black printer Andrew Cain; Black wagon drivers/couriers moving books and paper over hundreds of miles ("black jo the waggoner"); exploitative indenture contracts disguising sale as apprenticeship (12-year-old Cloe Morgan, sold twice); and two runaway Milton, MA papermakers — Caesar (recaptured, scalded legs from mill work) and James Moody (escaped, fought on the USS *Alliance*, used his prize money to buy out his own indenture). Reads flight/refusal itself as a form of labor critique — a strike against the book trade's dependency on Black bodies.
4. **"Critical Bibliography as Negative Dialectics"** — closing manifesto. "Book historians and bibliographers of the world—cut off your white heads!" Argues critique must act reciprocally on the scholar, not just the archive.

**Theoretical apparatus:** Kant's critique tradition; Foucault's "historical-philosophical" critique (*What Is Critique?*); Judith Butler, "Critique, Dissent, Disciplinarity"; Theodor Adorno's negative dialectics (nonidentity of concept and object, suspicion of reified belief systems, permanent social antagonism); Stephen Best, *None Like Us* (2018).

**Notable citations not yet in `BACKGROUND.md` before this pass (now added, §6):** Stephen Best, Marcy Dinius (*The Textual Effects of David Walker's "Appeal"*, 2022), Jordan Wingate ("Enslaved Pressmen in the Southern Press," 2022).

**Talking points for August:**
- Ask directly about *Excursion into the Interior* — it's the object his own scholarly identity is built around.
- His public critique of AAS's own 1983 *Printing and Society* volume signals he's willing to hold the institution accountable in print — a useful data point for how candidly he might discuss AAS's current gaps regarding early Black print.
- His method (financial paperwork as an archive not requiring the subject's literacy) is a concrete technique, not just a theoretical stance — worth asking whether AAS's own business/financial records (printer ledgers, subscription records) have been searched this way for the early Black press specifically.

## Cordell, "Speculative Bibliography"

**Full citation:** Cordell, Ryan. "Speculative Bibliography." *Anglia* 138, no. 3 (2020): 519–31.

**Updated 2026-07-20 from the complete text** (previously annotated from partial material — that caveat no longer applies).

**Core argument:** Digitization has not solved the scale problem in archives — it's restated it. Chronicling America holds ~16 million newspaper pages, but that's fewer than ten digitized New York City titles out of the hundreds or thousands that actually existed; the digitized archive is a small, uneven slice of the analogue one, and there is neither the time nor labor to comprehensively edit it the way Jerome McGann's "recurate and reedit" imperative demands. Cordell proposes *speculative bibliography* as a complementary response: using probabilistic computational models to constitute textual relationships — propositionally, iteratively, sometimes temporarily — rather than waiting for comprehensive manual editing that will never happen at this scale. The central claim: a computational model (e.g., the Viral Texts reprint-detection rule: 5+ matching 5-word phrases with 80%+ word overlap = "the same" text, clustered together) is not a neutral technical layer sitting beneath "real" bibliographic work — it **is** a bibliographic argument, exactly as much as a bibliographer's decision that two books belong together because they share a printer or a binding style. It's just executed at a speed and scale no individual human editor could reach.

**Structure:**

1. **"Recurated and Reedited"** — opens from McGann's *A New Republic of Letters* (2014) and its warning that mass digitization is dominated by for-profit actors and error-prone OCR pipelines built by technicians without historical training. Cordell's corrective: scale was never a purely digital-age problem — the analogue archive was never comprehensively curated either — so the "recurate everything" mandate is premature, not just impractical.
2. **"Toward Speculative Bibliography"** — defines the term and grounds it in the Viral Texts reprint-detection method, which is explicitly agnostic to author, title, or genre — categories mostly absent from both the original newspaper page and its digitized metadata.
3. **"Why 'Bibliography'?"** — bibliography, reframed, is "a system for modeling textual relationships" (by metadata *or* material features). Builds on Cordell's own prior essay "'Q i-jtb the Raven': Taking Dirty OCR Seriously" (*Book History* 2017): OCR output should be treated as a new *edition* in Tanselle's bibliographic sense (a copy from "a single job of typographical composition"), not a transparent surrogate for the print original — treating it as a mere surrogate "can encourage the devaluing (or even deaccessioning) of material holdings." Extends this to classification algorithms as a form of "offset editing" — researchers hand-tag a small training set, the algorithm operationalizes that model of relationship across millions of texts. Cites So and Long's finding that poems a classifier *mis*-identified as haiku actually revealed a real, previously unnoticed "Orientalist milieu" pattern in modernist poetry — i.e., classifier "errors" can be interpretively productive, not just noise to eliminate.
4. **"Why 'Speculative'?"** — situates the project between dehistoricized "big data" excitement and Katherine Bode's "scholarly edition of a literary system" (a curated dataset that replaces the curated text, explicitly interpretive and contingent rather than claiming direct/natural authority). Pulls in Drucker and Nowviskie's 2004 concept of "speculative computing" (branching, anticipatory processing) and — notably — **Bethany Nowviskie's 2016 "Speculative Collections," which draws on Afrofuturism** to argue digital collections should be built as "toolsets and resources for what could be," not just "statements about what was." Also cites **Lauren Klein's "Speculative Aesthetics" (*Early American Literature*, 2016)**, which proposes a speculative aesthetics specifically *for early American literary history* — eliciting knowledge about erased peoples and communities while explicitly marking what our distance from the past will always keep out of view.
5. **"Speculative Editions"** — describes the in-progress Viral Texts book *Going the Rounds* (University of Minnesota Press): each reprinted work gets a clean "anchor text" transcription that stays stable as a reference point even as the algorithm's clustering shifts with new data or tuning. Acknowledges the field has no "ground truth" dataset for 19th-century newspaper reprinting to validate against — the computational side is speculative too, not just the humanistic framing. Proposes the *speculative edition* — all texts associated through a single computational model — as a companion to Bode's "scholarly edition," explicitly **not** offered as literary-historical truth but as "propositions that demand testing and argumentation."

**Notable citations relevant to Felix's own reading list:** Katherine Bode, Ted Underwood (already assigned reading, field exam Area 4); Jonathan Senchyne's *The Intimacy of Paper* (2019) — a different Senchyne book than the one already in `BACKGROUND.md` (§6 currently cites his "Under Pressure" article), worth checking whether to add; Lauren Klein's "Speculative Aesthetics" (2016) — not yet in `BACKGROUND.md`, and directly on early American literary history specifically.

**The real finding — an unplanned convergence with Garcia:** Cordell reaches, via a completely different route (DH/Afrofuturism/speculative computing rather than critical theory), close to the same place Garcia does via Adorno and Stephen Best. Nowviskie's Afrofuturist reframing of archives as generative "toolsets for what could be" rather than fixed "statements about what was," and Klein's speculative aesthetics of eliciting knowledge about erased early American communities while marking the limits of recovery, are structurally very close to Garcia's negative dialectics — "knowing what withholds itself from the possibility of being known." Neither essay cites the other. This is a genuine, non-obvious bridge you'd be bringing into the room, not a forced comparison.

**Talking points for August:**
- Cordell's point that "the model is the argument, and it's contestable" is the right frame for describing what EBP-KG's inference rules (R1–R7) are doing — not asserting facts, but making auditable, challengeable claims, exactly the same posture Cordell describes for Viral Texts' reprint clusters.
- The Nowviskie/Klein thread (Afrofuturism, speculative aesthetics of early American absence) is worth raising directly with Garcia — it's a citation path his own essay doesn't take, and it sits closer to his negative-dialectics argument than anything else in Cordell's piece.
- Cordell's "Q i-jtb the Raven" point (OCR output is a new edition, not a surrogate — treating it as a surrogate risks deaccessioning) is a concrete, practical argument for why AAS's own OCR/digitization choices matter as scholarship, not just infrastructure — useful if the meeting gets into what's actually digitized vs. physically held.

## Two manifestos, side by side

| | Garcia | Cordell |
|---|---|---|
| Reorients bibliography around | Critique, reparation | Computation, probability |
| Core theorists | Kant, Foucault, Butler, Adorno, Stephen Best | Language modeling/NLP tradition, Bode, Drucker & Nowviskie, Nowviskie (Afrofuturism), Lauren Klein |
| What counts as evidence | Financial paperwork, indenture forms — archives that don't require the subject's literacy | Probabilistic patterns across millions of OCR'd pages |
| Stated method | Negative dialectics — respecting what "withholds itself from the possibility of being known" | Speculative modeling — propositional, iterative, contestable |
| On archival absence | Recovery without "critical fabulation" — negativity, not invention | Nowviskie/Klein: absence as generative — "toolsets for what could be," not just "what was" |
| Shared move | Both insist the *method itself* is a bibliographic argument, not neutral infrastructure underneath "real" scholarship — and both explicitly refuse to overclaim knowledge the record doesn't support | |

This is the frame worth bringing into the room: your dissertation and EBP-KG sit at the intersection of these two manifestos, applied specifically to the early Black press. Neither Garcia nor Cordell has written that intersection themselves — and the Nowviskie/Klein/Best convergence on archival absence is the most concrete, citable version of that intersection either essay gets close to.
