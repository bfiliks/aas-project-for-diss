## Garcia, "Critique! Critique! Critique! Black Labor in the Early American Book Trade"

**Full citation:** Garcia, John J. "Critique! Critique! Critique! Black Labor in the Early American Book Trade." *Criticism* 64, nos. 3–4 (Summer/Fall 2022): 317–32. Special issue: *New Approaches to Critical Bibliography and the Material Text*.

**Core argument:** Recovering Black labor in the early American/Caribbean book trade requires more than adding new subjects to bibliography's existing categories (author, editor, printer, publisher) — it requires bibliography to incorporate philosophical *critique* (Kant → Foucault → Butler) as a method, because the discipline's own presuppositions (what counts as evidence, whose labor counts as "the trade") are what occluded this labor in the first place. Garcia proposes "negative bibliography," drawing on Adorno's negative dialectics and Stephen Best's historicism of "knowing what withholds itself from the possibility of being known," as a way to do reparative scholarly work without either replaying archival violence uncritically or overclaiming knowledge the sources don't support (explicitly distances this from "critical fabulation").

**Structure:**

1. **Introduction** — states the manifesto directly: critical bibliography must critique its own field formation, not just widen its subject list. Notes the Andrew W. Mellon Society of Fellows in Critical Bibliography (Garcia is a member) avoids the word "critique" in its own mission statement — an internal jab at the field he's positioned within.
2. **"The Caribbean Reader"** — argues the British West Indies have been excluded from early American book history despite outsized print import volumes (only New England imported more books). Recovers enslaved domestic labor facilitating white plantation reading (Thistlewood's diaries), the first Jamaican printer (Robert Baldwin), and printer Alexander Aikman's own 1794 admission that production was slowed because "part of the business [was] being executed here by negroes" — direct documentary evidence of enslaved compositors. Centers a unique surviving illustrated book, *Excursion into the Interior from the Cape of Good Hope* (Jamaica, ca. 1799–1802, **held at AAS**), whose woodcuts stage colonial reading and critique together. Explicitly names AAS's own 1983 *Printing and Society in Early America* volume as having failed to synthesize prior Caribbean book-trade scholarship (Roderick Cave's work) — a critique of his own institution's founding paradigm.
3. **"Paper Trails"** — turns to US financial paperwork (ledgers, receipts, indentures) as an archive of Black labor that doesn't require literacy to recover. Key cases: two unnamed Black men paid to pull Philadelphia's 1788 Constitution-ratification "Printer's Float"; Mathew Carey's unnamed "resolute negro pressman" who struck over unpaid wages; retired 94-year-old Black printer Andrew Cain; Black wagon drivers/couriers moving books and paper over hundreds of miles ("black jo the waggoner"); exploitative indenture contracts disguising sale as apprenticeship (12-year-old Cloe Morgan, sold twice); and two runaway Milton, MA papermakers — Caesar (recaptured, scalded legs from mill work) and James Moody (escaped, fought on the USS *Alliance*, used his prize money to buy out his own indenture). Reads flight/refusal itself as a form of labor critique — a strike against the book trade's dependency on Black bodies.
4. **"Critical Bibliography as Negative Dialectics"** — closing manifesto. "Book historians and bibliographers of the world—cut off your white heads!" Argues critique must act reciprocally on the scholar, not just the archive.

**Theoretical apparatus:** Kant's critique tradition; Foucault's "historical-philosophical" critique (*What Is Critique?*); Judith Butler, "Critique, Dissent, Disciplinarity"; Theodor Adorno's negative dialectics (nonidentity of concept and object, suspicion of reified belief systems, permanent social antagonism); Stephen Best, *None Like Us* (2018).

**Notable citations not yet in `BACKGROUND.md` before this pass (now added, §6):** Stephen Best, Marcy Dinius (*The Textual Effects of David Walker's "Appeal"*, 2022), Jordan Wingate ("Enslaved Pressmen in the Southern Press," 2022).

**Talking points for August:**
- Ask directly about *Excursion into the Interior* — it's the object his own scholarly identity is built around.
- His public critique of AAS's own 1983 *Printing and Society* volume signals he's willing to hold the institution accountable in print — a useful data point for how candidly he might discuss AAS's current gaps regarding early Black print.
- His method (financial paperwork as an archive not requiring the subject's literacy) is a concrete technique, not just a theoretical stance — worth asking whether AAS's own business/financial records (printer ledgers, subscription records) have been searched this way for the early Black press specifically.

## Cordell, "Speculative Bibliography"

**Full citation:** Cordell, Ryan. "Speculative Bibliography." *Anglia* 138, no. 3 (2020): 519–31. (A related, more technical treatment — co-authored, heavier on NLP/language-modeling formalism — appears as an early section within a longer chapter on Manifold/University of Minnesota Press, reusing the same title and opening argument before extending into statistical methods this note doesn't cover.)

**Note on sourcing:** full text sits behind De Gruyter access I couldn't fetch directly; this annotation is built from the published abstract, Cordell's own description of the piece, and the framing material at the start of the related book-chapter section — not a read of the complete Anglia article. Worth actually reading in full via institutional access before citing it in the meeting.

**Core argument (as far as sourced):** Mass-digitized archives (millions of newspaper pages, etc.) have outgrown the possibility of comprehensive manual scholarly editing — the traditional bibliographic response to an unstable or partial textual record. Cordell proposes "speculative bibliography": using probabilistic computational models (language models, text-reuse detection) to *constitute* textual relationships — propositionally, iteratively, sometimes temporarily — rather than waiting to discover and fix them through edition-making. The key move: a computational model of, say, which newspaper texts reprint which others *is itself a bibliographic argument*, open to verification, amendment, or challenge on humanistic or computational grounds — not a neutral technical layer sitting beneath "real" bibliographic work.

**Supporting framing found in the related chapter material:**
- OCR-transcribed text should be treated as a primary source in its own right, not a transparent stand-in for the print original (citing Cordell's own 2017 work).
- Mass-digitized text is closer to raw manuscript witnesses than to a finished scholarly edition — genre, authorship, and even title are frequently unrecoverable from catalog metadata alone (Underwood's finding that searching "fiction" in library catalogs achieves only 37% recall is cited as an example).
- Positions itself in a lineage from early digital-editing projects (Rossetti Archive, Whitman Archive, Women Writers Project) and from Bode's work reconstructing a "stable and accessible representation of a historical literary system" out of serialized Australian newspaper fiction — i.e., speculative bibliography is offered as the successor move once full manual editing of an archive this size becomes impossible.

**Talking points for August:**
- This is Cordell's own field-reorientation manifesto, structurally parallel to Garcia's — one reorients bibliography around computation/probability, the other around critique/reparation. Worth naming this parallel explicitly rather than assuming Garcia will draw it himself.
- Cordell's point that "the model is the argument, and it's contestable" is a good frame for describing what EBP-KG's inference rules (R1–R7) are trying to do — not asserting facts, but making auditable, challengeable claims about relationships in the record.
- Worth getting full-text access before over-citing this one specifically — this annotation is built on partial material, unlike the Garcia annotation, which comes from the complete paper.

## Two manifestos, side by side

| | Garcia | Cordell |
|---|---|---|
| Reorients bibliography around | Critique, reparation | Computation, probability |
| Core theorists | Foucault, Butler, Adorno, Best | Language modeling / NLP tradition, Bode, Underwood |
| What counts as evidence | Financial paperwork, indenture forms — archives that don't require the subject's literacy | Probabilistic patterns across millions of OCR'd pages |
| Stated method | Negative dialectics — respecting what "withholds itself from the possibility of being known" | Speculative modeling — propositional, iterative, contestable |
| Shared move | Both insist the *method itself* is a bibliographic argument, not neutral infrastructure underneath "real" scholarship | |

This is the frame worth bringing into the room: your dissertation and EBP-KG sit at the intersection of these two manifestos, applied specifically to the early Black press. Neither Garcia nor Cordell has written that intersection themselves.
